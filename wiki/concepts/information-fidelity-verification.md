# Information Fidelity & Verification

## Definition
Information Fidelity & Verification is the principle that information quality varies greatly across sources, and that effective learning and decision-making require prioritizing sources that:
1.  **Verify facts** through methods like follow-up reporting ("后续").
2.  **Use official, primary data** (e.g., government statistics, research datasets).
3.  **Rely on expert documentation and peer-reviewed research.**

The goal is to avoid consuming distorted, low-fidelity, or algorithm-optimized content that sacrifices accuracy for engagement.

## The Fidelity Spectrum
Information exists on a spectrum from low to high fidelity:
- **Low Fidelity:** Social media summaries, clickbait headlines, AI-generated content without source verification, opinion pieces presented as fact.
- **Medium Fidelity:** News articles from reputable outlets, well-researched blog posts by experts, technical documentation from commercial entities.
- **High Fidelity:** Peer-reviewed academic papers, official government publications, data from original research, standards documents from international bodies, primary source historical documents.

The philosophy espoused in [[Zhihu]] argues that for substantive learning and professional development, one must consciously seek high-fidelity sources.

## Verification Practices
Key verification techniques include:
- **Tracing to Source:** Finding the original study, data release, or official statement behind a news claim.
- **Checking for Updates:** Looking for "后续" or follow-up reporting that confirms, corrects, or adds context to initial information.
- **Cross-Referencing:** Comparing information across multiple authoritative sources from different institutions or perspectives.
- **Evaluating Methodology:** For data and research, examining how the information was collected and analyzed.

## Connection to Curation and Infrastructure
This principle is the *why* behind [[Information Source Curation]] and a core value shaping the [[Self-Directed Learning Infrastructure]]. It provides the criteria by which sources are included or excluded from one's personal knowledge base.

The quote from [[James Gleick]] included in the source Zhihu discussion—"When information is cheap, attention becomes expensive"—encapsulates this challenge. Verification is the process of spending one's limited attention on high-fidelity information.
