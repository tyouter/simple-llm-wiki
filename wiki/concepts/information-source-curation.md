# Information Source Curation

## Definition
Information Source Curation is the systematic practice of identifying, evaluating, and organizing high-quality, authoritative information sources across different domains to facilitate efficient learning and research. It involves moving beyond algorithm-driven platforms and generic content aggregators to build a personalized library of trusted references.

## Core Principles
1.  **Authority Over Popularity:** Prioritizing sources based on expertise, institutional backing, and peer review rather than traffic or social media shares.
2.  **Primary Over Secondary:** Seeking original documents, data sets, research papers, and official documentation instead of summaries or interpretations.
3.  **Diversity of Perspective:** Intentionally gathering sources from different institutions, countries, and schools of thought to avoid echo chambers.
4.  **Continuous Maintenance:** Regularly reviewing and updating one's curated list of sources as new information emerges and old sources become outdated.

## Practical Application
As detailed in [[Zhihu Discussion: Comprehensive Guide to High-Quality Information Sources for Learning and Career Development]], effective curation involves:
- Building lists of authoritative websites for specific domains (e.g., .gov for statistics, .edu for academic content, official project docs for technical fields).
- Using tools like RSS readers to maintain direct pipelines to these sources, bypassing algorithmic feeds.
- Developing evaluation criteria to quickly assess the credibility of a new source.

## Relationship to Other Concepts
This practice is a foundational component of building a [[Self-Directed Learning Infrastructure]] and is driven by the need for [[Information Fidelity & Verification]]. It stands in contrast to passive information consumption models that rely on platform algorithms.
