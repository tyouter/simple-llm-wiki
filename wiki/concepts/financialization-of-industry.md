# Financialization of Industry

## Definition
The process by which financial markets, financial motives, financial institutions, and financial elites gain greater influence over the policy and outcomes of non-financial corporations. In the context of the Zhihu discussion, it specifically refers to the dominance of **Financial Logic** over **Industrial Logic**, leading to a prioritization of shareholder value metrics (stock price, dividends, buybacks) at the potential expense of long-term engineering quality, R&D investment, and operational resilience.

## Characteristics
1. **Shareholder Primacy:** Corporate governance focused primarily on maximizing shareholder returns in the short to medium term.
2. **Cost-Cutting for Margins:** Aggressive reduction of operational costs, including R&D and quality control budgets, to boost profitability metrics.
3. **Stock Buybacks & Dividends:** Using corporate cash flow for financial engineering to boost share price, rather than for reinvestment in the business.
4. **Executive Compensation Tied to Stock Price:** Aligning management incentives with short-term market performance rather than long-term product excellence.

## Case Study: Boeing
As cited in [[Zhihu Discussion: Financial vs. Industrial Logic in BYD's Development Strategy]], [[Boeing]] is presented as a canonical example. After its merger with McDonnell Douglas in 1997, the company's culture reportedly shifted from an engineering-centric focus to a financially-driven one. This is argued to have contributed to cost-cutting in critical areas, outsourcing of core competencies, and ultimately, the safety crises and reputational damage surrounding the 737 MAX.

## Contrast with Industrial Strategy
The discussion contrasts this trend with the approach of [[BYD (比亚迪)]] under [[Wang Chuanfu (王传福)]], which reinvests profits into technology (e.g., blade batteries) and [[Vertical Integration/闭环]]. The fear is that financialization erodes the [[Industrial Moats/护城河]] that make companies durable competitors.

## Debate
Critics of the strong anti-financialization argument contend that financial discipline is necessary to allocate capital efficiently and prevent managerial waste. The challenge is finding a balance that ensures accountability while fostering long-term innovation.
