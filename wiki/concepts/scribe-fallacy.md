# Scribe Fallacy

## Definition
An analogy used to argue against the fear that AI coding assistants will make software engineers obsolete. It compares the potential devaluation of manual coding skills to the historical fate of scribes after the invention of the printing press. The fallacy lies in believing that the *tool* (hand-copying manuscripts) was the scribe's core value, rather than their deeper skills in literacy, knowledge, and composition.

## The Analogy
- **Scribes (Pre-Printing Press)**: Valued for their ability to manually, beautifully, and accurately copy texts. The printing press commoditized and automated this mechanical act.
- **Software Engineers (Pre-AI)**: Often valued for their ability to manually, elegantly, and correctly translate logic into syntax. AI coding assistants are commoditizing and automating this mechanical act.

## The Core Argument
The Scribe Fallacy suggests that obsolescence fears focus on the wrong layer. The printing press did not make literacy or authorship obsolete—it amplified their importance and distribution. Similarly, AI coding tools like [[Claude Code]] are unlikely to make problem-solving, systems design, or product judgment obsolete. Instead, they may amplify the value of these higher-order **[[Engineer Identity Transformation|engineering skills]]** by freeing engineers from syntactic implementation.

## Application in the AI Coding Era
Discussed in the **[[Zhihu Discussion: Claude Code's Ecosystem, Impact on Engineering, and Practical Experiences]]**, the fallacy helps frame the current transition. The engineer's role is not disappearing but transforming from a "code scribe" to an "architect-orchestrator" who defines what needs to be built (**[[Commoditization of Execution]]**) and breaks down how to build it (**[[Decomposition as Core Skill]]**).
