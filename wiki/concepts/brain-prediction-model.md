# Brain Prediction Model

## Definition
A neuroscientific concept which posits that the primary function of the brain is **prediction**, not passive perception or understanding. The brain continuously generates a model of the world and predicts incoming sensory data. It then compares these predictions to actual sensory input.

## Core Mechanism
1. **Prediction Generation**: The brain uses prior knowledge (its internal model) to anticipate what will happen next.
2. **Prediction Error Calculation**: It calculates the difference between its prediction and the actual sensory signal.
3. **Error Resolution**: The brain must resolve this error. It has two primary options:
   - **Path 1 (Energy-Efficient)**: Reinterpret the sensory input to fit the existing model. (e.g., "I must have seen/heard that wrong.")
   - **Path 2 (Energy-Costly)**: Update the internal model to better predict future input.

## Role in [[Enlightenment (开悟)]]
As explained by [[杜千]] in the [[Zhihu Discussion on Enlightenment and Personal Transformation]], enlightenment is fundamentally linked to this model. The unenlightened state is characterized by a strong bias toward Path 1—prioritizing cognitive ease and consistency over accuracy. **Enlightenment is the cultivated tendency to choose Path 2**: to willingly engage in the metabolically expensive work of revising one's core worldview in light of new evidence.

## Implications for Learning and Belief
- **Confirmation Bias**: A natural consequence of the brain's preference for Path 1.
- **Cognitive Dissonance**: The uncomfortable state that arises when a large prediction error cannot be easily dismissed via Path 1.
- **Learning**: True learning requires the activation of Path 2, which is why it can feel effortful and destabilizing.

## Connection to the [[Three Stages of Understanding]]
- **Stage 1 (Acceptance)**: The brain operates heavily on pre-installed societal models with minimal error checking.
- **Stage 2 (Dissonance)**: Prediction errors become too large to ignore, forcing a confrontation with the model's inadequacy.
- **Stage 3 (Flexibility)**: The individual develops the skill to consciously select Path 2, making model updating a more routine process.
