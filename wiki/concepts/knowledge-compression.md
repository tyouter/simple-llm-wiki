# Knowledge Compression

## Definition
The cognitive process through which a well-understood procedure, derived from first principles, is internalized and practiced to the point of becoming automatic, fast, and effortless. It is presented as the sustainable alternative to "black-box" memorization.

## The Process
1.  **Derivation**: A concept or method is first thoroughly understood via [[Derivation-First Learning]].
2.  **Practice**: The derived procedure is applied repeatedly in various contexts.
3.  **Automatization**: Through practice, the procedure becomes "compressed" in the mind—shifting from a slow, conscious, step-by-step calculation to a fast, subconscious, almost intuitive operation.

## Key Metaphor: Hardware Acceleration
The author [[宙巡]] uses the analogy of "硬件加速" (hardware acceleration). Initially, the brain runs a general-purpose "software" algorithm (conscious derivation). After compression, it's as if dedicated "hardware" takes over, executing the task with high speed and low cognitive load.

## Contrast with Rote Memorization
| Aspect | Knowledge Compression | Rote Memorization |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Foundation** | Built on understanding and derivation. | Built on repetition without necessary understanding. |
| **Flexibility** | Retains connection to principles; can be adapted or debugged. | Brittle; fails if the problem deviates from the memorized pattern. |
| **Cognitive Load** | Low after compression (automatic). | Can remain high (requires active recall). |
| **Side Effects** | Strengthens the overall knowledge structure. | Can create [[Enumeration Path Dependency]] and waste cognitive "memory space." |

## Examples
- **Arithmetic**: A child who understands addition as combining quantities can, through practice, compress "7+8" into the automatic recall of "15," without having memorized it as a disconnected fact.
- **Algebra**: Deriving the quadratic formula multiple times leads to compression, where its application becomes swift, yet the learner still knows its origin.

## Role in the Learning Crisis
In the [[Zhihu Discussion: Learning Method Transition Crisis]], compression is presented as the goal. The crisis occurs when students try to skip the derivation step and go straight to memorizing the "compressed" outputs (formulas, answers) that others have created. This results in a fragile knowledge base lacking the underlying "source code."
