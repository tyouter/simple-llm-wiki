# Mental Model (心智模型)

## Definition
A user's internal, often subconscious, understanding of how a system, process, or product works. It encompasses their beliefs, expectations, and preconceived notions about causality, relationships, and workflows within a given context.

## Role in Product Design
A primary goal of user-centered design is to align the product's **conceptual model** (how the designer intends the system to work) with the user's **mental model**. A significant mismatch results in confusion, errors, and a perception that the product is "not intuitive."

## Connection to Scenario Analysis
As discussed in the [[Zhihu Discussion: The Role of 'Scenarios' in Product Management]], deep [[Scenario Analysis (场景分析)]] is a powerful tool for uncovering users' mental models. By observing and describing the complete **[[User Story vs. Environment|User Story]]**—not just the environment—designers can infer:
- How users expect a process to flow.
- What terminology they use to describe their goals.
- What they consider to be a "natural" grouping of functions or information.
- Their assumptions about cause and effect (e.g., "If I swipe here, it should...").

## Example
In the scenario of a user booking a ride-hailing service during a rainstorm:
- **Surface Need**: Get a car quickly.
- **Mental Model Revealed Through Story**: The user likely believes "drivers are scarce in bad weather" and "pricing will be dynamic." Their emotional state is anxious. A product aligned with this mental model might prioritize showing the number of nearby cars and surge pricing transparency *before* the booking confirmation screen, reducing anxiety and building trust.

Understanding mental models allows designers to create interfaces and workflows that feel familiar and logical to the user, reducing cognitive load and learning time.
