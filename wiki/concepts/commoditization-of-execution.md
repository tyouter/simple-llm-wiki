# Commoditization of Execution

## Definition
The process by which AI-powered tools make the act of translating intent or a plan into functional software code fast, cheap, and widely accessible. This reduces the competitive advantage and economic value derived solely from the ability to *build* software, shifting value upstream to activities like ideation, judgment, taste, and distribution.

## The Value Shift
- **From**: "How to build" (implementation skill, syntax knowledge).
- **To**: "What to build, for whom, and is it any good?" (product vision, user understanding, quality judgment, marketing).

## Implications for Engineers
As discussed in the **[[Zhihu Discussion: Claude Code's Ecosystem, Impact on Engineering, and Practical Experiences]]**, this commoditization is a primary driver of **[[Engineer Identity Transformation]]**. When execution is cheap, the engineer's unique value must come from other areas:
1.  **Problem Selection & Scoping**: Choosing the right problems to solve.
2.  **[[Decomposition as Core Skill]]**: Structuring problems for AI agents.
3.  **Systems Thinking & Integration**: Ensuring decomposed parts work together.
4.  **Validation & Taste**: Judging if the AI's output is correct, elegant, and meets user needs.

## Broader Business Impact
This trend lowers the barrier to entry for creating software, potentially increasing competition. Success becomes more dependent on non-technical factors like design, user experience, community building, and sales—areas where pure execution skill offers no advantage.

## Relation to Scribe Fallacy
The **[[Scribe Fallacy]]** argues that this commoditization of the *mechanical act* (coding/scribing) does not negate the value of the *profession* (engineering/literacy), but redefines its most valuable components.
