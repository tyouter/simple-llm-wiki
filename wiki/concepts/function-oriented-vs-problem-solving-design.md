# Function-Oriented vs. Problem-Solving Design

## Definition
A fundamental dichotomy in product philosophy that contrasts building products based on a list of features versus building products to solve specific user problems within authentic contexts.

## Function-Oriented Design
- **Focus**: Features, specifications, and technological capabilities.
- **Process**: Often starts with "What can we build?" or "What features do our competitors have?"
- **Mindset**: "Let's add a chat function," "Let's build a dashboard."
- **Risk**: Can lead to "feature bloat"—products with many functions that are rarely used because they don't address a core user need in a meaningful way. It often stems from a superficial understanding of context, focusing only on the **[[User Story vs. Environment|Environment]]** rather than the deeper user story.

## Problem-Solving Design
- **Focus**: User goals, pains, and the "job to be done."
- **Process**: Starts with deep user research and [[Scenario Analysis (场景分析)]] to understand "What problem are users trying to solve in this specific situation?"
- **Mindset**: "How can we help a remote team member feel connected and informed during asynchronous work?"
- **Outcome**: Leads to cohesive, focused products where every feature serves a clear purpose in a user's narrative. It is the philosophy advocated for in the [[Zhihu Discussion: The Role of 'Scenarios' in Product Management]].

## The Role of Scenario Analysis
Scenario analysis is the primary methodological bridge from function-oriented to problem-solving design. By forcing teams to articulate the complete **[[User Story vs. Environment|User Story]]**—including identity, motivation, and challenge—it shifts the conversation from "what features" to "what value." For example:
- **Function-Oriented**: "The smartwatch needs a heart rate sensor."
- **Problem-Solving (via Scenario)**: "For a new parent tracking their sleep deprivation, the watch needs to passively monitor heart rate variability to provide actionable insights on stress and recovery, not just raw data."

This distinction is central to modern product management and user experience design.
