# Elevator Effect/电梯效应

## Definition
A metaphor describing how the performance of an individual stock (or company) is often heavily influenced by the broader movement of its sector, the overall market, or macroeconomic trends—much like a person's vertical movement is determined by the elevator they are in, not just their own effort. The term highlights the limits of individual company agency in the face of powerful market cycles and sentiment.

## Explanation from the Zhihu Discussion
In [[Zhihu Discussion: Financial vs. Industrial Logic in BYD's Development Strategy]], the effect is used to explain the stock performance of companies like [[Geely (吉利汽车)]]. Despite the hard work of its founder [[Li Shufu (李书福)]] and aggressive expansion, Geely's stock price suffered significant declines. The author suggests this was partly due to the broader downturn in the automotive sector or negative sentiment towards Chinese stocks, factors outside Geely's direct control.

## Implications
1. **Market Inefficiency:** Suggests that stock prices do not always perfectly reflect company-specific fundamentals in the short to medium term.
2. **Challenge for Management:** Executives focused on industrial fundamentals ([[Industrial Logic]]) may feel frustrated when their company's stock price moves contrary to their operational achievements.
3. **Opportunity for Investors:** Presents potential buying opportunities when strong companies are undervalued due to negative sector-wide sentiment.

## Relationship to Core Concepts
The Elevator Effect is a consequence of the dominance of **Financial Logic** in market pricing, where narrative, sentiment, and macro flows can outweigh concrete industrial progress. It creates a disconnect that companies adhering strictly to **Industrial Logic** must endure.
